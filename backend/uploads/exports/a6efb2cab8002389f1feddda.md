#### **Project Description**
This project entails the detailing, fabrication, and erection of a 180-ton structural steel frame for a new three-story building. The structure's lateral stability is achieved through a system of steel moment frames, requiring specialized moment connections at critical beam-to-column joints. The design, materials, and workmanship will adhere strictly to the AISC 360-22 Specification for Structural Steel Buildings and the AISC 341-22 Seismic Provisions, as applicable. The scope includes all primary columns, beams, and girders required to complete the structural skeleton for subsequent trades.

#### **Key Quantities**
*   **Total Tonnage:** 180 tons (A992 Grade 50 steel)
*   **Total Pieces (Est.):** 2,000 – 2,300 members
*   **Primary Moment Connections (Est.):** 160 – 200 locations (requiring CJP welds or high-strength bolting)
*   **Simple Shear Connections (Est.):** 1,100 – 1,400 locations

#### **Top 5 Risks**
1.  **Connection Quality & Inspection:** Moment connections demand a high level of execution and rigorous inspection (e.g., Ultrasonic Testing). Insufficient welder qualification or inspection bottlenecks can cause significant schedule delays and costly rework.
2.  **Erection Tolerances:** The geometric control and plumbness of the moment frames are critical for fit-up and structural performance. Failure to maintain specified tolerances can cascade through the structure.
3.  **MEP Integration:** Deep moment frame beams and connection components can create conflicts with mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems. Late-stage coordination failures will result in expensive field modifications.
4.  **Skilled Labor Shortage:** The availability of certified field welders and ironworkers experienced with complex moment frame erection is a primary constraint that could impact both schedule and budget.
5.  **Material Lead Times:** Specific heavy wide-flange sections required for columns or long-span beams may have extended mill rolling schedules, delaying the start of fabrication.

#### **Top 5 Opportunities**
1.  **Connection Optimization:** Value engineer the specified moment connections. Exploring proprietary bolted systems (e.g., SidePlate, DuraFuse) or alternative welded details could drastically reduce field labor and inspection time.
2.  **Prefabrication / Modularization:** Maximize shop-welded sub-assemblies, such as "column trees," to minimize complex and weather-dependent field welding, improving quality and accelerating the erection schedule.
3.  **Early Steel Procurement:** Lock in material pricing by placing mill orders upon approval of advanced bill of materials, mitigating exposure to steel price volatility.
4.  **Advanced BIM Modeling:** Utilize a highly detailed (LOD 400) structural model to conduct clash detection with MEP and architectural designs, resolving all potential conflicts before fabrication begins.
5.  **Phased Erection Planning:** Develop a detailed erection sequence that stabilizes bays sequentially, allowing concrete and MEP trades to begin work on lower floors while steel erection continues above.
