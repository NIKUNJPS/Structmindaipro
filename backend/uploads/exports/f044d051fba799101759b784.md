### **1. Beam-Column Interior Joints (SMF Line)**

These connections are critical for the ductile performance of the moment frame and must accommodate large inelastic rotations.

*   **1.1. Recommended Connection Type:**
    **Bolted Flange Plate (BFP) Moment Connection.** This is a prequalified connection per AISC 358-22. It is chosen for its high ductility and superior constructability, as it eliminates field welding of the moment-resisting elements.

*   **1.2. Governing Limit States (AISC 360-22, Chapter J):**
    *   **Shear Transfer (via Shear Tab):**
        *   Bolt Shear Strength (J3.6)
        *   Bolt Bearing/Tearout on Beam Web and Plate (J3.10)
        *   Shear Yielding of the Plate (J4.2(a))
        *   Shear Rupture of the Plate (J4.2(b))
        *   Block Shear Rupture of the Plate (J4.3)
        *   Weld Strength (Shear Tab to Column Flange) (J2.4)
    *   **Moment Transfer (via Flange Plates):**
        *   Tensile Yielding of the Flange Plates (J4.1(a))
        *   Tensile Rupture of the Flange Plates (J4.1(b))
        *   Block Shear Rupture of the Flange Plates (J4.3)
        *   Bolt Shear Strength (J3.6)
        *   Bolt Bearing on Flange Plates and Beam Flange (J3.10)
    *   **Seismic Checks (AISC 341-22):**
        *   Connection must be designed for the Probable Maximum Moment (Mpr) of the beam.
        *   Column Panel Zone Shear Strength (E3.6f) is often a governing check.

*   **1.3. Bolt / Weld Sizing Guidance:**
    *   **Bolts:** Use high-strength ASTM F3125 Gr. A490 bolts for the flange plates. These must be installed as pretensioned and slip-critical to prevent slippage under load reversal. Shear tab bolts can be Gr. A325 installed snug-tight.
    *   **Welds:** The shear tab-to-column flange connection should be made with a CJP (Complete Joint Penetration) weld or double-sided fillet welds sized for the full shear demand. These are considered "demand critical" welds requiring stringent inspection.

*   **1.4. Constructability Score & Remarks:**
    *   **Score: 8/10**
    *   **Remarks:** Excellent choice for speeding up erection. All field work is bolting. Fabrication is straightforward but requires good tolerance control for bolt hole alignment. Eliminates costly and time-consuming field CJP welding and associated inspections.

*   **1.5. Alternate Solutions:**
    *   **Reduced Beam Section (RBS):** The classic "dog-bone" cut. Excellent, well-documented performance. Requires more precise fabrication and can sometimes interfere with floor decking or MEP.
    *   **Welded Unreinforced Flange - Welded Web (WUF-W):** A fully welded option. Very robust but has the lowest constructability score due to extensive field welding, inspection (UT), and weather sensitivity.

### **2. Brace-to-Gusset Connection (OCBF Line)**

This connection's primary function is to transfer large axial forces (tension and compression) from the brace into the frame.

*   **2.1. Recommended Connection Type:**
    **Bolted Brace to Welded Gusset Plate.** An HSS brace is bolted directly to a gusset plate, which is in turn welded to the beam and/or column. This is the industry standard for efficiency and reliability.

*   **2.2. Governing Limit States (AISC 360-22, Chapter J):**
    *   **Brace:** Tensile Yielding (D2a), Tensile Rupture (D2b), Block Shear Rupture (J4.3), Compressive Strength (E3/E4).
    *   **Gusset Plate:**
        *   Whitmore Section Yielding and Buckling (J4.4)
        *   Block Shear Rupture at the brace interface (J4.3)
        *   Gusset Plate Edge Buckling (AISC Design Guide 29)
    *   **Bolts:** Bolt Shear Strength (J3.6), Bolt Bearing/Tearout on Brace and Gusset (J3.10).
    *   **Welds (Gusset to Frame):** Weld strength (J2.4), designed to resist the force resultant determined by the Uniform Force Method (UFM).

*   **2.3. Bolt / Weld Sizing Guidance:**
    *   **Bolts:** ASTM F3125 Gr. A325 bolts are typically sufficient. Since this is a bearing connection, they can be installed snug-tight. Bolt layout should be compact to minimize gusset size.
    *   **Welds:** Use fillet welds to connect the gusset to the beam/column flanges. Weld size is often 5/16" or 3/8" and can be determined directly from the UFM analysis.

*   **2.4. Constructability Score & Remarks:**
    *   **Score: 9/10**
    *   **Remarks:** Highly constructible. This is a standard, repeatable connection that fabricators and erectors are very familiar with. The main consideration is providing adequate clearance for tightening the bolts.

*   **2.5. Alternate Solutions:**
    *   **End Plate Brace Connection:** Can simplify gusset design but requires more fabrication on the brace itself and tighter tolerances. Not as common for standard OCBF systems.
    *   **Pinned Connection:** Using a single large pin can be effective but is generally more complex and expensive to fabricate.

### **3. Column Base Plates**

The base plate design depends entirely on its location within the mixed system.

*   **3.1. Recommended Connection Type:**
    *   **SMF Columns:** **Fixed Moment Base Plate.** Requires a thick base plate, a large pattern of heavy anchor rods, and often shear keys to transfer moment, shear, and axial load into the foundation.
    *   **OCBF/Gravity Columns:** **Pinned Base Plate.** A simpler connection designed primarily for shear and axial load, with minimal moment resistance. Anchor rods are typically clustered near the column web.

*   **3.2. Governing Limit States (AISC 360-22, J8 & ACI 318-19, Ch. 17):**
    *   **Fixed Base:**
        *   Concrete Bearing Strength (J8)
        *   Base Plate Bending at the faces of the column/stiffeners (AISC DG 1)
        *   Anchor Rod Tensile Strength (ACI 318)
        *   Concrete Breakout/Pullout Strength of Anchors (ACI 318)
        *   Weld Strength (Column to Base Plate) (J2.4)
    *   **Pinned Base:**
        *   Concrete Bearing Strength (J8)
        *   Anchor Rod Shear Strength (ACI 318)
        *   Concrete Edge Breakout in Shear (ACI 318)

*   **3.3. Bolt / Weld Sizing Guidance:**
    *   **Anchors:** Use ASTM F1554 Gr. 55 or 105 for fixed bases to provide high tensile strength in a smaller diameter. Use double nuts (one for leveling, one for tightening) for moment transfer. Gr. 36 is often sufficient for pinned bases.
    *   **Welds:** CJP or double-sided PJP welds are required for column-to-base plate connections at SMF columns. Fillet welds are typically sufficient for pinned bases.

*   **3.4. Constructability Score & Remarks:**
    *   **Fixed Base Score: 6/10**
    *   **Pinned Base Score: 9/10**
    *   **Remarks:** Fixed bases are challenging. Anchor rod placement is critical and unforgiving. Leveling and grouting thick plates is a slow, precise operation. Pinned bases are simple and fast.

*   **3.5. Alternate Solutions:**
    *   **Proprietary Anchoring Systems:** Some systems (e.g., Peikko, Hilti) offer pre-engineered solutions that can improve tolerance control and speed up installation, but may come at a premium cost.

### **4. Column Splices**

Splices are required to join column sections between floors. Their design is driven by constructability and the forces they must transfer.

*   **4.1. Recommended Connection Type:**
    **Bolted Flange and Web Plate Splice.** Plates are shop-welded to the bottom of the upper column segment and field-bolted to the top of the lower segment. The splice is typically located ~4 ft above the finished floor for ease of access.

*   **4.2. Governing Limit States (AISC 360-22, Chapter J):**
    *   Bolt Shear Strength (J3.6)
    *   Bolt Bearing on Plates and Column Flanges/Web (J3.10)
    *   Gross and Net Section Yielding/Rupture of Splice Plates (J4.1, J4.2)
    *   Block Shear Rupture of Plates and Column Sections (J4.3)
    *   Compressive Bearing Strength of Column Ends (J1.4(c)) (if not a complete tension splice).

*   **4.3. Bolt / Weld Sizing Guidance:**
    *   **Bolts:** ASTM F3125 Gr. A325 bolts installed in a bearing-type connection are usually sufficient. In SMF columns, design forces should consider potential moments from frame action.
    *   **Location:** Placing the splice 4 ft to 6 ft above the floor keeps workers off ladders and allows for simple, safe connection work.

*   **4.4. Constructability Score & Remarks:**
    *   **Score: 8/10**
    *   **Remarks:** Very common and efficient. The use of erection aids like guide lugs is highly recommended. Requires coordination to ensure crane can place the upper column segment while erectors install bolts.

*   **4.5. Alternate Solutions:**
    *   **Directly Welded Splice:** Provides a clean, flush aesthetic but is very expensive and slow due to field welding, NDT inspection, and weather delays. Generally avoided in commercial construction.
    *   **Proprietary Splices:** Some systems offer quick-connect mechanical splices, but they are less common and may have specific design limitations.
